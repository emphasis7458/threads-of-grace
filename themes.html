Thematic Pathways

Explore Pat's meditations by spiritual theme

While Pat's meditations follow the liturgical calendar, certain themes weave through the entire collection—threads of grace that reappear across seasons and years. These thematic pathways offer another way to engage with her contemplative wisdom.

Each theme gathers meditations that address a particular aspect of the spiritual life. Click any theme to explore recommended readings.

The Contemplative Life

Silence & Stillness

Finding God in quiet

+

Pat returns again and again to silence as the foundation of contemplative life. Drawing on Thomas Keating's insight that "silence is God's first language," she invites readers to discover the peace that comes from simply being still in God's presence.

"Be still and know that I am God." Try that for a minute and see what you notice. If that minute of silence whetted your appetite, try it tomorrow for two minutes, and the next day for three. Likely you'll be surprised by the peace that infuses you.

— "Peace! Be Still!" (June 20, 2021)

Presence & Awareness

Noticing God in the present moment

+

God is always here, but we may not be—distracted by yesterday's regrets or tomorrow's worries. Pat draws on Brother Lawrence's "Practice of the Presence of God" to teach awareness of the Divine in ordinary moments. The contemplative life is not about adding more religious activities but about waking up to what is already true.

God is here right now. It is in the present moment where we may encounter the Holy One. Unfortunately, like Martha in today's gospel lesson, we are often so "distracted by our many tasks" that we don't notice the Divine Presence in our midst.

— "Distracted" (July 21, 2019)

Prayer

Conversation with the Beloved

+

Pat approaches prayer not as performance but as relationship. Using the ACTS framework (Adoration, Confession, Thanksgiving, Supplication), she shows how prayer encompasses the full range of human experience. What matters is not the form but the intention—drawing near to the One who is already near.

A full prayer life is broad and varied and encompasses both prayers in community and personal prayers. It doesn't matter how you pray, what you bring to the conversation. The important thing is to commit time to strengthening your relationship with God through prayer.

— "Prayers, Corporate and Individual" (September 19, 2010)

Lectio Divina & Scripture

Letting the Word seep into our being

+

Pat teaches scripture engagement as more than reading—it's encounter. Through lectio divina (the Benedictine practice of sacred reading) and Ignatian imaginative prayer, we allow God's Word to become living and active in us. Like cows chewing their cud, we return to the text throughout the day, digesting its nutrients.

The Benedictine way, known as lectio divina, takes a word or phrase from the passage that gets our attention and uses it as a focus for meditation, contemplation, letting the word seep into our being.

— "Are You Listening?" (February 17, 2013)

The Inner Journey

True Self & False Self

Discovering who we really are in God

+

From birth, we construct a false self—a persona to protect us from life's wounds and win approval from the world. But deep within lies the true self, the imago dei planted by God, waiting to emerge. The spiritual journey involves the gradual dismantling of our protective walls so the true self can breathe and grow.

Our ego has been at work, since before we can remember, building this fortress to protect us from the slings and arrows of fortune... We come to realize that we are imprisoned in walls of our own making and that, to be free, we must let go of our stones one by one.

— "Not One Stone" (November 14, 2021)

Surrender & Letting Go

Releasing our grip into God's hands

+

Surrender is not defeat but liberation. Pat speaks from personal experience—"surrender was certainly the first step for me on my spiritual journey"—and acknowledges that "surrender is never once and done." Using Mary's fiat as the model, she invites readers to release attachments, control, and self-protection into God's hands.

If we want to deepen our relationship with the Beloved, that's just what we are called to do—surrender ourselves into God's hands—Mary's "yes" to the Angel Gabriel is our model.

— "Laid Bare" (October 10, 2021)

Transformation

Being changed from the inside out

+

Transformation is God's work within us—usually slow, often imperceptible, but real. Pat shares her own story of being brought to her knees at mid-life and the decade-long process of becoming someone new. Using Lazarus as a metaphor, she describes how the Holy Spirit removes our bindings "first one and then another, freeing us for an ever more spacious new life."

I wasn't a completely different person the next day or even the next year, but after ten years I could look back and see how different I had become.

— "Transformation" (October 1, 2017)

Dark Night & Wilderness

Finding God in absence and desolation

+

Spiritual dryness, God's apparent absence, times of transition and loss—these are not signs of failure but part of the journey. Drawing on John of the Cross, Pat reframes the dark night as gift: "a time to come to know God in intangible absence as well as tangible presence." The wilderness is where transformation happens, where we move "from order through disorder to reorder."

Could this so-called outer darkness be what John of the Cross called the "Dark Night of the Soul"?... Might it be a time for being stretched to come to know God in intangible absence as well as tangible presence, a time to awaken to a new level of spiritual growth?

— "Outer Darkness" (October 11, 2020)

God and World

Divine Love & Mercy

The heart of God revealed

+

At the center of Pat's theology is the conviction that "God is utterly self-giving love." She cherishes the Hebrew word hesed—God's steadfast love and mercy—and traces how divine love flows through creation, redemption, and our daily lives. This love is not earned but given; grace is "a pure, undeserved gift from the Beloved."

God is utterly self-giving love. We come to know that in the perichoresis of the Trinity, the circular dance of love of the Father, Son, and Holy Spirit.

— "Everything" (November 7, 2021)

Union & Indwelling

God's dwelling in us and we in God

+

Union is God's desire—union with God, with one another, with all creation. Pat traces the biblical witness that we are temples of the Holy Spirit, that Christ lives in us, that we are rooted and grounded in divine love. This is not distant theology but intimate experience: "We are each and all... houses, dwellings, temples, abiding places for the Holy One."

Union is God's desire for us, union with God, union with one another throughout the world, union with all creation, all interconnected through the mystery of God's loving presence.

— "Union or Separation" (October 4, 2009)

Compassion & Justice

Love moving into the world

+

Contemplation is not escape from the world but engagement with it. Pat traces how God's compassion flows through us to become "God's hands and feet in our world today." Jesus's mission statement—good news to the poor, release to captives, justice for the oppressed—remains our call. As we grow in awareness of divine love, we naturally become more other-centered.

May we be open and available to be God's compassionate hands and feet in our world today.

— "Compassion" (June 9, 2013)

Unity & Peace

The oneness of all in God

+

Pat holds a cosmic vision: all creation interconnected in God's love, the blue marble of Earth seen from space, the mystical insight that "thou canst not stir a flower without troubling of a star." This unity is not rational but experiential. And from this awareness flows peace—not the world's peace (absence of conflict) but God's peace that remains unshaken in the midst of turmoil.

We are each and all mutually interdependent, part and parcel of the unity of our One creating, redeeming, sustaining God. God's unity is not rational, only experiential.

— "Unity" (June 7, 2020)
